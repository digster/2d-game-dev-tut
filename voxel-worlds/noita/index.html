🎯 What's Different Here?

💡 The core distinction: in Terraria voxels, tiles are passive — they sit in place until the player digs or places. In Noita voxels, every pixel runs a simulation step every frame. Sand falls. Water spreads. Fire propagates. Gases rise. Materials react on contact. The data structures, the update order, and the performance constraints are all genuinely different from Terraria voxels — which is why this is a separate sub-track rather than a tier inside Terraria.

🎬 The final sandbox (by the end of the Advanced tier)

  • A pixel-grid canvas rendered straight to an ImageData buffer
  • Sand that piles up and slides into gaps
  • Water that flows sideways and pools at low points
  • Oil that floats on water (density-ordered)
  • Fire that ignites wood and oil, burns down, spawns smoke
  • Gases that rise and react with materials they touch
  • A material library where adding a new material is a single table entry

🧪 What This Sub-Track Covers

Concepts unique to cellular-pixel voxels — none of this is in the Terraria sub-track:

1. Cellular automaton per pixel

The atomic operation: look at a pixel and its 8 neighbors, decide its next state. Done naïvely you get a single-frame ripple bias (everything falls left because we read left-to-right). We fix that with double buffering and a checker-pattern update order.

2. The falling-sand engine

The canonical rule that powers half the genre: down → down-left → down-right. We'll derive it, see why the order matters, and what happens when you flip it. This is the seed every other material rule mutates.

3. Liquid & gas density rules

Water adds a sideways-spread step to sand. Oil floats on water by being lighter — implemented as a density comparison during the swap rule. Gases invert gravity and behave just like liquids upside down.

4. Fire propagation as multi-step CA

Fire isn't one rule — it's a stack: probabilistic ignition of neighboring fuel, a burn counter that decrements per frame, smoke spawning above hot cells, and finally turning burnt material into ash. Multi-step rules are the difference between "looks like sand" and "looks like Noita".

5. Material reaction tables

The bit that makes the sandbox feel alive: when material A touches material B, both transform into C and D. water + lava → steam + stone. acid + steel → rust + gas. A reaction is just a lookup-table entry; the engine that consults the table is one shared loop. This is where adding "one new behavior" doesn't grow code proportionally.

6. Active-cell tracking (performance)

Updating every pixel every frame is wasted work — most of the world is settled. We track "dirty" rectangles and "sleeping" cells so the CA only touches what's actually moving. This is different from Terraria's chunking because here the cells update themselves; chunking is about scheduling, not just streaming.

🔁 What This Sub-Track Does NOT Re-Cover

Several voxel fundamentals carry over from Terraria verbatim. Rather than re-teach them, we link back. Make sure you're comfortable with these from the Terraria sub-track before starting:

  • Tile / pixel coordinates and screen math → see Terraria Beginner — Tile coords. Identical, just at pixel granularity.
  • Scrollable camera + viewport culling → see Terraria Beginner — Camera.
  • Grid storage in a flat Uint8Array → see Terraria Beginner — Data structures. Same shape, same indexing math.
  • Chunking and persistence → see Terraria Expert / Advanced tiers (coming soon). The mechanics carry over; the granularity changes (pixel vs tile) but the chunk-streaming pattern is the same.
  • Tile-based lighting / flood fill → see Terraria Advanced tier (coming soon). Noita uses the same flood-fill idea at pixel granularity.
