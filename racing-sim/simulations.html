⭕ Slip Ratio vs Slip Angle & the Friction Circle

The Expert tier introduced slip angle (α) — the angle between the velocity vector and the heading. That governs lateral force. There's a matching number for longitudinal force called slip ratio (κ):

📐 Slip ratio (longitudinal)

κ = (ω · r − v) / v

where ω is the wheel's angular velocity, r the wheel radius, and v the forward speed. When κ > 0 the wheel is spinning faster than the ground (wheelspin under throttle); when κ < 0 it's spinning slower (lockup under braking). Both peak at some small slip ratio — typically 0.1–0.15 — and fall off past the peak, same shape as the slip-angle curve.

🔵 The friction circle

A tire can produce at most some maximum total force Fmax. Lateral and longitudinal force together must fit inside a circle (or, in real life, an ellipse) of radius Fmax:

Flat² + Flong² ≤ Fmax²

That's why "trail braking" works (some of the grip budget that was longitudinal becomes lateral as you ease off the brake into the corner), and why "powering through" a corner with full throttle and full steering doesn't (you're asking for ~1.4× Fmax — the tire just slides). It's also why ABS exists — locking the wheels puts you at 100% longitudinal grip, 0% steering. Refresh the Expert tier's Pacejka section here.

🌡️ Tire Heat & Wear

A racing tire is a chemical compound that only works in a narrow temperature window. Below it the rubber is hard and slips; above it the rubber's gone gummy and slips. Every pro driver spends the first lap warming the tires (weaving, late braking) and the last lap nursing them (smooth inputs, no slides). We model this with a single temperature → grip multiplier curve per tire.

📐 The simplest tire-temp model

  • Each tire has a temperature in °C, starting at ambient (say 25°).
  • Slip generates heat: ΔT/Δt = kheat · |slip|.
  • The tire also bleeds heat to the surrounding air: ΔT/Δt = −kcool · (T − Tambient).
  • Grip multiplier is a bell curve centred on the optimal temp (say 95°). Below 60° or above 130° = 60% grip; in-window = 100% grip.

✈️ Aerodynamic Downforce

A wing makes a car heavier the faster it goes. That extra "weight" is purely a downward aerodynamic force from air pushing on inclined surfaces. It scales with speed squared — at 200 km/h there's 4× as much downforce as at 100 km/h.

📐 The downforce equation

Fdown = ½ · ρ · v² · A · CL

where ρ is air density (~1.225 kg/m³), v is speed (m/s), A is the wing area, and CL is the wing's lift coefficient (negative for downforce). For a fast F1 car at 300 km/h, Fdown can exceed the car's weight — which is why they're often described as being able to drive upside down on a ceiling.

In a top-down sim, downforce is a scalar that multiplies the tire's peak force D. More downforce → more grip → tighter cornering. That's it.

🧷 Suspension as Spring + Damper

A car body is a mass riding on four springs (the suspension). When a wheel hits a bump, the spring compresses and pushes the body up; the damper (shock absorber) dissipates kinetic energy so the body doesn't keep bouncing. The whole assembly is a classic damped harmonic oscillator — refresher in the Fundamentals: Spring Physics & Damping.

📐 The ODE

m·ẍ + c·ẋ + k·x = Froad(t)

where x is the body's vertical displacement, m is the body mass, k the spring stiffness, c the damping. The dimensionless damping ratio ζ = c / (2·√(k·m)) classifies the response:

  • ζ < 1 — under-damped: oscillates before settling. Comfortable; bad over bumpy tarmac.
  • ζ = 1 — critically damped: returns to zero in minimum time, no overshoot. The "sweet spot" for a road car.
  • ζ > 1 — over-damped: returns slowly, no overshoot. Feels wooden; great for absorbing big single hits.

📊 Live g-g Diagram & Telemetry Traces

A g-g diagram is the iconic engineering plot from every motorsport telemetry tool. Lateral acceleration on the X axis, longitudinal acceleration on the Y axis. A pro driver's plot traces the rim of the friction circle — they're using all the grip all the time. An amateur's plot is a small blob in the middle.

📐 Reading a g-g plot

  • Top half — accelerating (positive long. accel).
  • Bottom half — braking (negative long. accel).
  • Left/right — left/right cornering.
  • Diagonal quadrants — trail braking (bottom-left/right) and power-on cornering (top-left/right).
  • Hugging the circle's edge — driving at the limit of grip.

Drive a car & watch the plot

The dashed circle is the friction limit — stay inside, or the dot turns red. The three telemetry traces below show speed and the two accel components over time.

🗺️ Procedural Track Generator

Every track in this tutorial so far has been a hand-authored oval. Generating arbitrary closed tracks from a seed unlocks endless content. The trick: start with a circle of evenly spaced points in polar coords, perturb each point's radius using a seeded PRNG, then run each pair of consecutive points through a smoothing pass (we use cubic Béziers tangent to the local circle direction). Refresher: Procedural Generation.

📐 The recipe in three steps

  1. Polar points: N points evenly spaced around a circle, each at radius R · (1 + jitter · rand(−1, +1)). Higher jitter = more wiggly.
  2. Bézier handles: for each pair of neighbours, pick handle positions perpendicular to the local radial direction so the joined curve is smooth.
  3. Sample: walk the cubic Bézier chain to produce a centerline, then inflateWalls() as usual.

Drag the seed slider to step through random tracks. Complexity changes the number of control points; jitter changes how wiggly the track becomes. A small auto-driver follows the generated centerline so you can see if it's drivable.
